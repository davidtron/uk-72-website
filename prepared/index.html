What to do when there's problems.

Making a plan and being prepared are key to resilience. Here's some tips on how to prepare, what to do if disaster strikes and how to get back to normal as soon as possible afterwards.

Flood advice.

Think about flooding before it happens. Over five million people live in flood risk areas in the UK, so make sure you are prepared. Sign up for free flood warnings and create a personal flood plan.

  • Before a flood
  • During a flood
  • After a flood
Print Full Guide

  1. Call Floodline.

    Check if your home is at risk. Floodline provides warning information and general advice 24 hours a day on 0345 988 1188.

  2. If you are at risk.

    Choose furnishings that can be easily removed to store at higher levels.

  3. Prevent floodwaters.

    Buy unfilled sandbags and fill with sand from builders merchants. Consider other types of barriers that might prevent flood water coming into your home through airbricks, doors and windows.

  4. Learn how to turn off your mains supply.

    Find out how to turn off your electricity, gas and water in advance.

  1. Alert neighbours.

    Check that elderly and vulnerable people are ok if its safe to do so.

  2. Don't walk, swim or drive through floodwater.

    Keep your family and pets indoors and away from flood waters. Floods are dangerous, you can get swept over in as little as six inches and the water may be contaminated with sewage.

  3. Don't walk on riverbanks and sea defences.

    Don't be tempted to see where the flooding is occurring as the water can rise rapidl.y

  4. Turn off mains supplies.

    Turn off gas, electricity and water.

  5. If you need to evacuate.

    If the emergency services ask you to leave, shut your windows and lock doors. Take your pets and supply bag. The council will find you somewhere on a temporary basis.

  1. Only return once safe.

    Take care if driving or walking through floodwater for hidden dangers such as raised manhole covers. Do not step in puddles of standing water if power lines are down outside your home. If you smell gas or a hissing noise call the fire service.

  2. Check for safety.

    Don't use electrical and gas appliances until they have been declared safe. Boil tap water until the water supply has been declared safe for consumption.

  3. Contact your insurer.

    Take photos that clearly show the water damage to your home and contents. Do not start cleaning until they have assessed the damage.

  4. Cleaning up.

    After the insurer completes their assessment, ensure all standing water has been removed particularly under the floors. Remove all damaged carpets but keep samples for the insurers. Wear protective clothes and a standard face mask while disinfecting all affected areas with a brush, hot soapy water and disinfectant.

Fire.

There are thousands of house fires each year, many of which are preventable.

  • Before a fire
  • During a fire
  • After a fire
Print Full Guide

  1. Fit a smoke detector on every floor.

    Check the smoke alarm every week and never disable them

  2. Take extra care in the kitchen.

    Make sure appliances such as hobs are turned off after use.

  3. Turn off portable heaters.

    Switch off portable heaters when you leave the room or go to bed.

  4. Be careful with naked flames.

    Take care to extinguish candles, cigarettes and fires.

  5. Don't overload sockets

  6. Plan an escape route and make sure everyone knows it.

    Make sure its kept clear at all times and door keys are easy to find.

  1. Get out and stay out.

    Don't enter a burning building.

  2. Call emergency services.

    Dial 999

  3. Leave safely.

    If a door feels hot don't open it. Never use a lift.

  4. If trapped.

    If smoke, heat or flames block your exit stay in the room with the door closed. Put a wet towel under the door and call 999 Wave brightly coloured cloth or use a torch to signal for help

  1. Confirm it's safe.

    Never re-enter your home until the fire service has confirmed it's safe.

  2. Contact your insurance company as soon possible.

    You may need to arrange temporary accommodation. Contact the mains gas, electricity and water suppliers.

Motorway breakdown.

  • Before a breakdown
  • During a breakdown
  • After a breakdown
Print Full Guide

  1. Service your car.

    Prevention is much better than waiting for a breakdown. Ensure your car is serviced regularly

  1. Don't stop.

    Only stop if its a real emergency. Try to drive to a safer place off the motorway rather than stopping on the hard shoulder

  2. On the hard shoulder.

    If you must stop pull over as far left as possible. Leave sidelights on and switch on hazard flashers.

  3. Don't stand on the hard shoulder.

    Fast moving traffic won't see you. Keep passengers and yourself away from the carriageway and hard shoulder behind the barrier or up the bank. Don't attempt even simple repeairs

  4. Give an accurate location.

    Walk to your nearest emergency telephone, or describe your location using reference numbers on all telephone marker posts

  1. Build up speed before pulling onto carriageway.

    Before rejoining, build up speed on the hard shoulder and watch for a safe gap in the traffic Be aware that other vehicles may be stationary on the hard shoulder

Chemical Emergencies.

  • Before
  • During a chemical emergency
  • After an environmental leak
Print Full Guide

  1. Keep chemicals safe.

    Keep medicines and cleaning products out of site and reach of children.

  2. Keep in original containers.

    Never decant into soft drink bottles, someone might accidentally drink them.

  3. Next mix products.

    Some combinations can create toxic gases.

  4. Don't leave spills.

    Clean up spills immediately with rags. Be careful to protect your eyes and skin.

  1. Call emergency services.

    Call on 999

  2. Seek expert advice.

    Don't give anything by mouth. Unless advised by medical profesional

  3. Move away from the chemicals.

    Move away from immediate source of chemical danger.

  4. Know what you are dealing with.

    Keep the label of the chemical with you to show to emergency services.

  1. After a spill.

    If dangerous chemical released into environment listen to the news and follow instructions

  2. Wait inside.

    If told to wait inside put tape around closed doors, windows and air vents.

Drought.

  • Before a drought
  • During a drought
  • After a drought
Print Full Guide

  1. Get into good habits

    Peel and wash fruit and veg in a bowl of water rather than running tap

  2. Turn off tap while brushing teeth.

    It can save up to 5 litres a minute.

  3. Dont use clean water for non essential tasks.

    You can water the garden, clean windows with grey water (from bath or appliance) or save rain water in a watter butt.

  1. Follow advice.

    Always follow advice regarding hosepipe bans

  1. Remember.

    Continuing to use water sensibly is good for the environment and society.

Flu.

Influenza causes many deaths annually and can infect millions of people in pandemic.

  • Before
  • During
  • After a flu pandemic
Print Full Guide

  1. Coughs and sneezes.

    Use a clean tissue when you cough, sneeze and blow your nose. Bin after use.

  2. Wash your hands.

    Regularly wash your hands with soap and hot water or a sanitiser gel.

  3. Clean hard surfaces.

    Keep door handles, remote controls and computer mice clean.

  1. Stay at home.

    During a pandemic you will be advised to stay indoors. If in doubt call your GP or the NHS on 111.

  1. Stay healthy.

    Eat a balanced diet, drink plenty of fluids, exercise daily, get enough rest and sleep.

Heatwave.

A heatwave is an extended period of hot weather relative to the expected conditions of the area at that time of year. For some people a hot weather can be dangerous and cause health problems after one or two days of a temperature rises.

  • Before a heatwave
  • During a heatwave
  • After a heatwave
Print Full Guide

  1. Plenty of water.

    Make sure you take plenty of water especially when travelling.

  2. Plan to keep cool.

    Work out which room is coolest, shade your windows and buy an electric fan.

  3. Stay safe.

    Learn about first aid and heatstroke. Prevent sunburn with a suncream of at least SPF 15 with UVA protection.

  1. Keep out of the heat.

    The hottest part of the day is between 11am and 3pm. Keep your living space cool especially for infants and older people, turn off non essential electrical equipment.

  2. If you do go out.

    Wear sunscreen of at least SPF 15 with UVA protection. Wear UV sunglasses and a hat.

  3. If someone feels unwell.

    Move them to a cooler place and offer them water or fruit juice to drink.

  4. Avoid extreme physical exertion.

    Wait till the cooler parts of the day and take frequent breaks.

  5. Dont' leave children or pets in closed cars.

    The temperature can rise rapidly.

  1. Stay healthy.

    Eat a balanced diet, drink plenty of fluids, exercise daily, get enough rest and sleep.

Power Cut.

  • Before a powercut
  • During a powercut
  • After a powercut
Print Full Guide

  1. Find where your fusebox is.

    When the power goes out you need to check if it's a problem in your house or a power cut

  2. Have a windup torch in your emergency pack.

    When the power is out you'll need a torch.

  1. Check if your neighbours have electricity.

    If they don't, it's likely that the fault is on the electricity network. Contact your provider, don't assume that they will know that your power has gone off.

  2. Prevent a spike.

    Turn off and unplug all electrical equipment. Sensitive electronics can be damaged when the electricity comes back on.

  3. Leave a light on.

    So you know when the power is back on.

  4. Check when the power may be restored.

    You can check your outages here

  1. Stay safe.

    Do not touch downed electrical power lines and keep your family and pets away.

  2. Check your freezer.

    If food in freezer is colder than 4degrees C and has ice crystal on it, you can refreeze it.

Winter weather.

  • Before a cold spell
  • During a cold spell
Print Full Guide

  1. Check weather warnings.

    Check for severe weather warnings for your area.

  2. Stay warm.

    Before winter sets in make sure your home heating is working efficiently. Consider portable gas or oil heaters for emergencies.

  3. Plan alternative ways to stay warm.

    If your power goes out make sure you have extra blankets, sleeping bags and hot water bottles.

  4. Set your timer if away.

    If you are away during cold weather set your timer to come on occasionally.

  1. Check regularly on elderly neighbours.

    See if they need help staying warm, getting in provisions and keeping their pathway clear of snow and ice.

  2. Wrap up warm.

    Most body heat escapes from your head. Wear a hat to stay warm.

  3. Prevent burst pipes.

    Keep your thermoset set at a steady temperature day and night. Running water is harder to freeze, so if necessary leave it trickling to prevent the pipes freezing.

  4. Keep stoves ventilated.

    Fuel burning equipment should be vented to the outside.

  5. Avoid driving.

    Consider if the journey is necessary. If so pack ready to eat food, a warm drink in a flask. Bring warm clothes and blankets. If you break down stay with your vehicle.

Storm.

Print our storm Guide to learn what to do.

  • Before a storm
  • During a storm
Print Full Guide

  1. Keep an eye on weather forecasts.

    Postpone outdoor activities if thunderstorm is imminent.

  2. If you are outdoors seek shelter if possible.

    Once you hear thunder you are already in range of lightening strike.

  1. Shut doors and windows.

    Stay inside during the storm and away from windows

  2. Avoid the phone and taps.

    Telephone lines and metal pipes can conduct electricity

  3. If outside.

    Find a low lying open place that is a safe distance from water, trees, poles or metal objects.

  4. If you feel your hair stand on end.

    Squat close to the ground with your hands on your knees and your head tucked between them. Try to touch as little of the ground as possible.

  5. If driving.

    Stop and park away from trees and overhead power lines.

Terrorist attack.

Terror attacks can happen without warning but you can prepare for peace of mind.

  • Before attack
  • During attack
  • After attack
Print Full Guide

  1. Be vigilant.

    Attacks would normally happen in public places. Keep an eye out for suspicious behaviour, vehicles or packages. Call the police anti-terror hotline on 0800 789 321 if you have doubts.

  2. Know your exit.

    In buildings and transport be aware of the emergency exits.

  1. Get out.

    Look for the safest way out of a building or away from the vicinity. Leave as quickly and calmly as possible.

  2. If inside and there falling items.

    Shelter under a sturdy table. If trapped, stay close to the wall. Don't use lighters or matches as there may be gas leaks.

  3. Alert the police.

    If you saw the explosion or any suspicious behaviour, tell the police what you saw.

  1. Help others.

    In the immeidate aftermath, help others if you can give first aid and support.

  2. Volunteer information.

    Tell the police any information you think may be helpful.
